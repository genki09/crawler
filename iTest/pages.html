Japanese Ceremonies and Festivals

  Many people agree that Japan is a modern country. During the early days of last century, Japanese absorbed lots of new ideas from the western countries. At the same time, it keeps its traditional cultures very well. Though like other Asian countries, Japanese traditional culture is deeply influenced by the Chinese culture, it also has many distinct features. The Japanese people are very proud of their culture. Here are some introductions about the interesting ceremonies and festivals in Japan.

  Coming of Age Ceremony

  The coming of age is the moment at which a teenager legally becomes an adult. In Japan this is at the age of 20.

  Since 1948, the ceremony has been held on the Coming of Age Day, the second Monday of January (the day used to be on January 15 until the year 1999). The day is a national holiday and local governments generally hold some ceremony. Females tend to wear a traditional Japanese formal dress. After this age, the right to smoke, drink and vote is granted to them. It was known as genpuku (元服礼) among samurai (日本武士) in the past.

  Genpuku in Japan was a celebration that showed a samurai was considered to be an adult. The age of it varied roughly from 12 to 18. They usually changed their names from their birth names to adult names, changed their hairstyles and started to be treated as an adult, for example, by separation from their mother or other family members. Some were even given a territory to protect. No samurai was allowed to marry before this ceremony. Females did not have genpuku or any equivalent ceremony.

  Doll Festival

  The Doll Festival in Japan is on the 3rd of March. On this day, people display dolls dressed in old-style Japanese clothes and have a sweet drink which is made from rice.

  It is a celebration especially for girls. Formerly people believed the dolls possessed the power to conceal bad spirits in their bodies and would save the owner from dangerous encounters.

  The origin of this festival is about a place where paper dolls are put into a boat and sent on a journey down a river, taking with them the bad spirits.

  Japanese New Year

  In ancient times, Japanese New Year was the same as Chinese or Korean New Year. Today, January 1st is New Year's Day for Japan. It is one of the most important festivals of the whole year. New Year's Day is a traditional festival which has been celebrated for centuries. That is why there are many small customs that are still carried on in many families.

  The Japanese people eat a special selection of dishes on New Year's Day. Some of the popular food included are sticky rice cakes, vegetables, black beans and so on. Many of the dishes are sweet and raw fishes are often eaten as well. The table for New Year's Day is one of the joys for many Japanese.

  Like the Chinese people, on New Year's Day, the Japanese people have a custom of giving pocket money to children. It is given in small decorated envelopes. The amount of money given depends on the age of the child.

  Another custom of Japanese is making rice cakes. Boiled sticky rice is put into a wooden shallow bucket like container and patted with water while another person hits it with a large wooden hammer. By hitting the rice repeatedly, the rice gets sticky and forms a sticky white dumpling. This is made before New Year's Day and eaten during the beginning of January.

  White Day

  In Western countries, people celebrate Valentine's Day (情人节) on February 14. Lovers can send gifts to each other to show their love. Especially, women send chocolates to their beloved ones while receiving flowers. In Japan, besides the Valentine's Day, lovers celebrate another festival called White Day.

  White Day is celebrated on March 14, one month after Valentine's Day. On White Day, men who received chocolates on Valentine's Day return the favor and give gifts to the women who gave them chocolates on Valentine's Day.

  Traditional Festival for Children

  A traditional festival day in Japan for children aged three, five and seven is on the 15th of November. Children who are aged three, five and seven will go to a temple or a holy place to drive out evil spirits. Old Japanese believe that at the age of odd number years, which are up to 13, something important will happen to the child.

  Today, boys who are aged three and five, girls who are aged three and seven will dress up in traditional Japanese clothes and have fun on this day. They will eat a kind of candy, and the adults will wish them a long and happy life.

  Cultural Festival

  Most schools in Japan, from junior high schools to universities, have an annual event called Cultural Festival. At the Cultural Festival, students display their everyday achievements. People who want to enter the school themselves or are interested in the school may come to see what the schoolwork is like, and what the school atmosphere is like. Parents may also want to see what kind of work their children have been doing at school.

  However, many people who visit cultural festivals come just for fun. Much food is served at cultural festivals, and often classrooms or gymnasiums are transformed into temporary restaurants or cafes. There are also performances such as dances, concerts and plays. These are performed by individual volunteers or by various school clubs, such as the dance club and the drama club.

  The Cultural Festival is a very fun event, but this is not the only reason why it attracts so many visitors. The Cultural Festival is the only opportunity throughout the entire year that allows students to see what life is like in other schools. The Cultural Festival makes people's lives richer by increasing interaction.
